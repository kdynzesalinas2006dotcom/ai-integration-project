Identity & Access Management (IAM)

Definition

IAM is a cybersecurity framework that controls access to systems, cloud apps, networks, and assets based on each user or device's digital identity. It covers identifying users, assigning roles, granting/removing access, and maintaining secure authentication.

Why it matters

IAM balances security and usability: it enforces least-privilege access, supports MFA and SSO, and helps teams provision, update, and deprovision accounts to reduce unauthorized access risk.

Real-world example

The Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack (DarkSide) exploited credentials and access weaknesses, encrypting systems and disrupting fuel distribution—highlighting why strong IAM and MFA matter for critical infrastructure.

Real job

IAM Analyst — manages authentication support, MFA/SSO tools, account recovery, log monitoring, and enforces secure access controls across an organization.

Endpoint & Mobile Security

Definition

Endpoint security protects devices (laptops, desktops, mobile phones) that connect to an organization's network. Each device is an access point and potential vulnerability, so endpoints must be secured to protect the whole network.

Why it matters

Endpoints are often the weakest link and prime targets for attackers seeking sensitive data or a foothold into networks. Securing and monitoring endpoints reduces breach risk.

Real-world example

In 2018 a laptop containing over 33,000 Canadians' health data was stolen and found to be unencrypted—showing how unsecured endpoints can lead to major data breaches.

Real job

MGR - Telecom & Endpoint — manages endpoint lifecycle, MDM, patching, compliance, VoIP hardening, and coordinates with cybersecurity teams to keep devices secure.

How AI helped

Cryptography

Definition

Cryptography is the practice of encoding information so only intended recipients can read it. It uses algorithms, math, and engineering to protect confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity of data in storage and transit.

Why it matters

Cryptography prevents unauthorized access to sensitive information and enables secure communications, e-commerce, and compliance with privacy laws (e.g., HIPAA in healthcare).

Real-world example

Healthcare organizations rely on cryptography to protect patient records and communications. Proper encryption is required for HIPAA compliance and to prevent data breaches.

Real job

Application Security Engineer — ensures secure use of encryption, hashing, and cryptographic best practices in applications to protect data at rest and in transit.

How AI helped

AI helped locate authoritative sources about cryptography and verify that the job responsibilities match cryptography-related tasks such as secure coding, key management, and encryption use.

Incident Response

Definition

Incident response is an organization's process for detecting, isolating, and recovering from cybersecurity incidents. It limits damage and restores operations when breaches, malware, or other security events occur.

Why it matters

Without a coordinated incident response, organizations suffer longer downtime, greater data loss, and increased legal and reputational damage. Preparedness shortens recovery time and limits impact.

Real-world example

CISA responded to an agency where EDR alerts revealed exploitation of a GeoServer vulnerability (CVE-2024-36401). Attackers used the flaw to access multiple servers over weeks before detection.

Real job

Major Incident Manager — coordinates and leads the response to major incidents, ensures communication, engages technical teams, and drives service restoration following ITIL practices.

How AI helped

AI supported source discovery, validation, and helped summarize incident response procedures and job alignment in concise language.
